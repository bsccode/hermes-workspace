# Question 1 — Jurisdiction and Standing (Draft)

Issue

Whether the Minister’s decision to declare the Dungeons & Dragons club (D&D Inc) a "problematic group" under s 2 of the Kill Bill Act 2030 (Cth) is a decision to which the Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act 1977 (Cth) ("ADJR Act") applies — in particular whether the Federal Court has jurisdiction to entertain an application for review under the ADJR Act and whether the Applicant (D&D Inc) has standing to bring that application.

Short answer (conclusion)

Yes — on the facts given the Minister’s declaration is an administrative decision made under statute that affects the legal interests and obligations of D&D Inc and therefore falls within the ADJR Act’s scope. D&D Inc has standing: it is directly affected (a club registered with the university, subject to deregistration, fines and sanctions) and therefore satisfies the ‘‘special interest’’/person aggrieved threshold recognised in the authorities and explained in this unit’s lectures (see lecture notes, Judicial Review/Standing). The Court therefore has jurisdiction to hear the matter under the ADJR Act (subject to other threshold issues which you were asked not to address).

Rule

1. ADJR Act jurisdictional threshold
- The ADJR Act permits an application for review in respect of a "decision to which this Act applies" (ADJR Act, s 5). In practice, courts ask whether:
  (a) the impugned act or decision is a governmental decision made under an enactment; and
  (b) it is of an administrative character affecting the rights, privileges, obligations or interests of a person (see the ADJR Act and standard judicial exposition in administrative law texts and lectures).

2. Standing — the ‘‘special interest’’ / person aggrieved
- Standing to bring judicial review is narrower than mere public interest. The High Court in Australian Conservation Foundation v Commonwealth (1980) 146 CLR 493 emphasised that an applicant must show some special interest beyond that of the public at large to obtain standing (the ACF 'special interest' principle).
- Federal courts have applied the ACF test and more flexible approaches in subsequent cases; the lecture slides emphasise that the Federal Court may adopt a slightly more lenient approach (special/pecuniary or direct interest), and that businesses or organisations with a direct statutory or practical interest (e.g. a registered club facing deregistration or sanction) will usually qualify (see lecture notes: standing under the ADJR Act and cases discussed in lectures).

Application

1. Is the Minister’s declaration a "decision" to which the ADJR Act applies?
- The declaration is expressly made under the Kill Bill Act 2030 (Cth) — a federal enactment — and has immediate legal consequences for the Applicant (threat of deregistration, fines and suspension). The lecture material (ADJR Act, s5; jurisdiction: administrative character) identifies such statutory declarations as classic examples of reviewable administrative action.
- The decision is therefore: (i) made under an enactment; and (ii) of an administrative character (it is not merely advisory or preliminary) because it directly changes the legal status and entitlements of the club.
- On these facts the declaration squarely falls within s 5’s ambit.

2. Does D&D Inc have standing?
- The Applicant is a registered club that has been declared a "problematic group" and informed by the university that the club will be deregistered and that the Minister has been notified. The declaration directly affects the club’s legal status, membership rights, ability to meet on campus, and exposes it to statutory sanctions and fines. These consequences are immediate, concrete and specific to the Applicant. That is exactly the type of "special interest" that the High Court and Federal Court have treated as sufficient for standing.
- The ACF principle denies standing to mere public-spirited litigants who do not suffer special disadvantage; D&D Inc is not in that position. Its register, operations and finances are directly at risk.
- The lecture notes highlight authorities where commercial or business interests, or organisations with a direct operational interest, have been held to have standing — the facts here (risk of deregistration, sanctions) place D&D Inc comfortably within the class of persons entitled to bring an ADJR Act application.

Practical issues and counter-arguments

- The Respondent might argue that the Minister’s declaration is not a "decision" for the purposes of s 5 (for example, by characterising it as a non-reviewable political act). That argument is unlikely to succeed where there are express statutory consequences (deregistration, fines) and where the decision is framed and implemented in statutory terms.
- The Respondent might contend D&D Inc lacks standing because the Minister’s information-gathering was preliminary or because a university internal process should be exhausted first. But the ADJR Act and the courts permit review of statutory decisions that produce immediate legal consequences, especially where internal avenues offer no effective remedy or where the statutory declaration itself is the decisive administrative act.

Conclusion and relief sought

- The Federal Court has jurisdiction under the ADJR Act to hear an application challenging the Minister’s declaration. D&D Inc has standing: it is directly affected and therefore entitled to bring the ADJR Act application.
- Relief sought (practical recommendation): an interlocutory injunction to halt deregistration or enforcement action pending determination of the substantive judicial review arguments; declaratory relief that the declaration is ultra vires (if that ground ultimately succeeds); and consequential relief (costs, orders for reasons, and any other appropriate remedies).

## Slide 4

Definition of ‘officer’ and ‘director’

S 9 Corporation Act 2001 (Cth) defines an ‘officer’ to include:
A director or company secretary; or
A person:
Who makes, or participates in making decisions that affect the whole, or substantial part, of the business of the company
Who has the capacity to affect significantly the company’s financial standing, or
In accordance with whose instructions or wishes the directors of the company are accustomed to act
An officer of the company includes:
A receiver, or receiver and manager of the property of the company
An administrator of the company; or 
A liquidator

NB: It is important to appreciate the definition of officer because statutory duties are applicable to them – in Morley v ASIC [2010] a chief financial officer, joint company secretary and general counsel were held to be officers of the company since they participated in decisions which affected the business of the company in whole or substantially. 
SEE: S9AC and S9AD

Director

Defined under s 9AC Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) as:
(1)  A director of a company or other body is:

(a)  a person who:

(i)  is appointed to the position of a director (de jure); or

(ii)  is appointed to the position of an alternate director and is acting in that capacity;

regardless of the name that is given to their position; and

(b)  unless the contrary intention appears, a person who is not validly appointed as a director if:

(i)  they act in the position of a director (de facto); or

(ii)  the directors of the company or body are accustomed to act in accordance with the person's instructions or wishes(shadow director) (excluding advice given by the person in the proper performance of functions attaching to the person's professional capacity or their business relationship with the directors or the corporation).

## Slide 8

Role of Directors

ASIC V Healy [2011] (Centro Case)
Concerned the responsibility of directors of a large listed company in approving the company accounts
In that case, they failed to disclose material matters in the accounts
Middleton J stated:
(a) directors are important component of corporate governance. They are placed at the pinnacle of the structure of direction and management of the company. The higher the office the greater the responsibility. Their role is important as their action has a profound impact on the community and not just shareholders, employees and creditors
(b) a board should be established which enjoys the varied wisdom, experience and expertise of person drawn from various background. Despite the background, a duty greater than that of simply representing a particular field of experience or expertise is imposed. A director will not be relieved of the duty to pay attention to the affairs of the company which may reasonably be expected, to attract inquiry, even if it was outside of the directors expertise. \

Board Procedure

Directors Meeting
Directors pass resolutions in board meetings
The rules to this is found in Corporations Act or company constitution
Must be more than an informal meeting, Petsch v Kennedy [1971] discussion between directors was not an effective directors’ meeting unless the directors’ were so aware
Resolutions
RR248G – directors’ resolution must be passed by a majority of the votes cast by directors entitled to vote on the resolution
Chair has casting vote – S248G(2)
Resolutions without meetings (circular) – RR248A – directors sign off the document
Notice of Board Meetings – RR248C serve reasonable notice. The frequency of meetings is dependent on the company, except for publicly listed companies, which is governed by ASX Rules
Quorum – the minimum number may be stated by the company constitution, or RR248F provides 2 directors for a valid meeting, and the quorum must be present throughout the meeting.
Minutes – proceedings of meetings must be recorded in the company’s minutes book.

## Slide 31

The duties are owed to whom?

The duties are owed to the company and not to individual members, creditor or others.
However, this does not mean the directors must not take cognizance of their interests, but must regard them as a whole when the company deals with them.
Can duties be owed to an individual member?
In an exceptional situation, this may happen.
Brunninghausen v Glavanics [1999] 32 ACSR 294
B and G were the only directors and shareholders of the company. Though so G did not actively participate in the management of the company. A dispute ensued, which resulted in an agreement that G will sell his shares to B and resign as a director. However, B did not tell G that there was an offer for all the shares of the company, which was then sold for a profit. In this case the courts held that B owed duties to G.
Why: Because B held a position of considerable advantage and thus owed a duty not to place his interest above that of others, and by not disclosing the intended sale, he has breached this duty. B took advantage of G’s lack of knowledge for his benefit
Analysis: This case may be limited to a situation where a member is so reliant on a director for information and advice that a court may be persuaded to hold that a director owed a duty to the individual member. (since there was a relationship of trust and confidence).

## Slide 32

Duty of Care

Sources of this duty
S180(1) Corporation Act 2001
Contract between the director and company – a term o f the contract may incorporate that the director must exercise care and diligence in occupying the position
General law-  under the law of negligence directors will owe a duty of care to their companies to exercise reasonable care. 
(S179 – both general and statute law applies)
S180(1) states that 
(1)  A director or other officer of a corporation must exercise their powers and discharge their duties with the degree of care and diligence that a reasonable person would exercise if they: 
(a)  were a director or officer of a corporation in the corporation's circumstances; and 
(b)  occupied the office held by, and had the same responsibilities within the corporation as, the director or officer.

## Slide 33

Elements to establish duty of care under s180(1)

The breach is determined by answering the following question:
Test: Would a reasonable person in the company’s circumstances and holding the position and responsibilities of the defendant director (officer)  would have done what the defendant director (officer) have done?
If answer is yes – the defendant director has exercised care, skill and diligence
If the answer is no – the defendant director has not exercised care, skill and diligence

## Slide 35

Reasonable Care

A director is required to exercise the degree of care that a reasonable person would exercise in the company’s circumstances and holding the same responsibilities as the director concerned.
Daniels v AWA Ltd
Facts: AWA 25% profits from foreign exchange currency trading. Company officer in charge of this Koval (K) was allowed to operate without effective supervision. K hid losses through unauthorized loans (total $50 mil). All this time, Daniels (D) (accountant) contracted to audit company accounts and internal processes – didn’t report K’s actions/deficient supervision to AWA Directors. AWA sued D for negligence. D sue directors for contributory negligence.
ISSUE: Are AWA Executive Directors and Non-executive Directors in breach of duty of skill, care and diligence?
HELD: Executive Directors – yes (fail to put effective supervision to ensure proper audit). Non-executive Directors – no.
NED: reasonable to believe internal controls are fine + have confidence in senior management
ED: had reliable info re-deficiencies  & fail to put effective supervision to monitor proper audit and follow up info (rather than rely on info of sources to say it is alright)

## Slide 36

Skill

Skill expected of a director is dependent on the following factors:
Type of director – executive or non-executive;
Possession of any special qualifications
Must have skills necessary to have a basic understanding of the business of the company and its financial status
Will be tested objectively: Whether a reasonable director with similar expertise and in a similar position and circumstances exercise their skill in a similar manner?
ASIC v Vines 
Mr. Vines was the Chief Financial Officer of the GIO Group but not a director. He failed to disclose relevant information to GIO when it was his duty to do so. It was held that Mr Vines ought to have considered whether to disclose material information of which he was aware. The findings show that in each case, he had acted in a manner which had the effect of excluding the disclosure of material information. Mr Vines' failure to provide the directors with that material information left them to make decisions on the basis of significantly incomplete facts . Further he contended that he relied on information furnished by another executive.
It was held: that he should have checked the accuracy of the information and in this instance Vines was judged on the basis of what a reasonably competent chief financial officer would have done.

Would have to exercise the degree of diligence that a reasonable person would exercise if they were a director of a company in the company’s circumstances and same responsibilities.

## Slide 38

Delegation and Reliance

To what extent can directors delegate the functions to others and rely on those persons for the performance of the same.
S198D allows directors to delegate their powers unless restricted by the constitution. 
The person to whom the powers are delegated must exercise it in accordance to the direction and effectively. 
What if the delegate was negligent in exercising the delegated powers by the directors?
S190             
(1)  If the directors delegate a power under section 198D, a director is responsible for the exercise of the power by the delegate as if the power had been exercised by the directors themselves. 
(2)  A director is not responsible under subsection (1) if: 
(a) the director believed on reasonable grounds at all times that the delegate would exercise the power in conformity with the duties imposed on directors of the company by this Act and the company's constitution (if any); and 
(b)  the director believed: 
(i)  	on reasonable grounds; and 
(ii)  	in good faith; and 
(iii)  	after making proper inquiry if the circumstances indicated the need for inquiry; 
that the delegate was reliable and competent in relation to the power delegated.

## Slide 39

SECT 189 - Reliance on information or advice provided by others                   
If: 
(a) 	a director relies on information, or professional or expert advice, given or prepared by: 
(i)  an employee of the corporation whom the director believes on reasonable grounds to be reliable and competent in relation to the matters concerned; or 
(ii)  a professional adviser or expert in relation to matters that the director believes on reasonable grounds to be within the person's professional or expert competence; or 
 (iii)  another director or officer in relation to matters within the director's or officer's authority; or 
 (iv)  a committee of directors on which the director did not serve in relation to matters within the committee's authority; and 
                     (b)  the reliance was made: 
(i) 	in good faith; and 
(ii)  after making an independent assessment of the information or advice, having regard to the director's knowledge of the corporation and the complexity of the structure and operations of the corporation; and 
(c)  the reasonableness of the director's reliance on the information or advice arises in proceedings brought to determine whether a director has performed a duty under this Part or an equivalent general law duty; 
the director's reliance on the information or advice is taken to be reasonable unless the contrary is proved. 

ASIC V Healey
Failure to disclose short-term liabilities by classifying them as non-current liabilities.
Held: Breached duty of care and diligence as directors take overall responsibility for the company’s financial report. Omission should have been obvious to the directors, given their knowledge and experience. Directors could not substitute reliance upon others for their own attention and examination of an important matter that falls specifically within the Board’s responsibilities, such as reporting obligations. The Act places a specific task on the Board and each director: approving the financial statements. Consequently, each member of the board was charged with the responsibility of attending to and focusing on these accounts and, under these circumstances, could not delegate or ‘abdicate’ that responsibility to others.

## Slide 43

RECENT CASES

Cassimatis v Australian Securities and Investments Commission [2020] FCAFC 52:The Full Federal Court recently dismissed an appeal by Emmanuel and Julia Cassimatis, the former directors of Storm Financial, against findings by Edelman J (before he was appointed to the High Court) that they breached their statutory directors' duty of care.
Facts: The appellants, in this case, Mr and Ms Cassimatis, were the founders, sole shareholders, and executive directors of Storm Financial Pty Ltd (Storm). Storm held an Australian Financial Services Licence (AFSL).  
Storm's main activity was advising clients to invest in accordance with the 'Storm model', which was both designed by and very closely overseen by the appellants. The Storm model involved borrowing on the family home and entering into margin loans to invest in index funds. Once initial investments were made, clients would be encouraged to make 'step' investments over time. ASIC alleged that this advice was provided to all clients on a 'one size fits all basis' irrespective of the individual circumstances of the clients.  
According to the Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC), by the time of Storm's collapse in early 2009, approximately 3,000 of its 14,000 clients had been 'Stormified’, and some had sustained significant losses. 
Held: By a majority of two to one, the Full Court upheld the 2016 decision of Justice James Edelman that Emmanuel and Julie Cassimatis breached their duty of care as directors of Storm Financial.

## Slide 44

Cassimatis v Australian Securities and Investments Commission [2020]

The decision relates to the directors of Storm Financial Pty Ltd (Storm). The Court made the following general observations as to the content of the statutory duty to exercise due care and diligence:
The “corporation’s circumstances” include all relevant commercial and other circumstances, including any breach or risk of potential breach of the law by the corporation;
All the responsibilities of the director are to be considered regardless of how or why they have them. These responsibilities are not limited to statutory obligations imposed on the director under the Act, but also include whatever responsibility the director has within the corporation; and,
Duty of care and diligence is normative, and its burden is a matter of public concern, not just private rights.
Ratification by the shareholders does not absolve an impugned director for a breach of his or her duty under the Act.

## Slide 45

Cassimatis v Australian Securities and Investments Commission [2020]

“…shareholders cannot sanction, ratify or approve, qua themselves as directors, their own conduct in contravention of s180. Nor can they release themselves from such a contravention. That follows because of normative, objective, irreducible standard of care and diligence directors must live up to…”.
In assessing the directors breach of s180 duty, the foreseeability of a risk that the company may contravene the law is to be considered when assessing the factual matrix relevant to the company’s circumstances under section 180 (1) of the Act “but the liability of the director under s180(1) is direct and not derivative from the company’s contravention.”

## Slide 47

Elements of the duty

Liability is imposed if the following can be satisfied:
The person was a director at the time when the debt was incurred;
The company was insolvent or became insolvent due to the debt;
When debt was incurred there were reasonable grounds to suspect that company was insolvent or would become insolvent due to the debt; and
Director was aware at the time debt was incurred there were reasonable grounds to suspect the company was insolvent or a reasonable person in a similar position in the circumstances of the company would have been so aware.

## Slide 50

All the other elements

Insolvent -  95A – a company is insolvent when a company is unable to pay its debts as and when it becomes due
Suspect insolvency - Even if a company is insolvent when incurring the debt, a director will not be automatically in breach of s588G as there must be reasonable grounds for suspecting insolvency and this will be judged according to a director of ordinary competence who is capable of having a basic understanding of the company’s financial status.
It has to be more than a mere speculation – there must be positive reason to suspect insolvency.
Director aware –if a director actually was aware that there was reasonable ground to suspect insolvency or where a reasonable director in his position and circumstances would have reasonably suspected insolvency – this requirement can be satisfied. See Metropolitan Fire Systems Pty Ltd v Miller

## Slide 53

Metropolitan Fire System v Miller

Facts: Raydar Pty Ltd went into liquidation owing money to Metropolitan Fire Systems Pty Ltd for the installation of fire prevention systems and prevention. Raydar’s directors were Miller and Lewis.
Cause of Action:  MFS commenced proceedings against Miller and Lewis, claiming they had allowed Raydar to trade while insolvent, thereby breaching s588G. Miller and Lewis relied on the S588H defence.
Held: Miller and Lewis’s expectation that Raydar was solvent was wishful thinking rather than a reasonable expectation of solvency.
Raydar was insolvent when it incurred debt with MFS
Defence failed.

## Slide 56

Safe Harbour Defence s588GA and s588GB

In order for a director to have the benefit of the safe harbour are: 
the director starts to suspect that the company may become or be insolvent; 
 the director starts developing one or more courses of action that are reasonably likely to lead to a better outcome for the company (need to demonstrate that the better outcome is one that is better for the company than the immediate appointment of an administrator, or liquidator, of the company) ; and
 the debt is incurred directly or indirectly in connection with any such course of action.

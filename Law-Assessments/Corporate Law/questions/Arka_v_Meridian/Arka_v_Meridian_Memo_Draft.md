    16|## Analysis — Issue 1: Authority and Ostensible Agency
    17|
    18|### Rule
    19|- Actual authority (express or implied) — a principal is bound by acts of an agent if the agent had authority to enter the contract.
    20|- Apparent/ostensible authority — where a principal’s representations lead a third party to reasonably believe the agent has authority: see *Freeman & Lockyer v Buckhurst Park Properties* [1964] 2 QB 480; *Royal British Bank v Turquand* (the "indoor management" rule) and corporate constitution issues.
    21|- Constitutionally required execution formalities — if the company constitution requires two directors or one director + company secretary (and this is a mandatory constitution requirement), a deed signed only by one director may be invalid/unauthorised.
    22|
    23|### Application
    24|
    25|- Rohan's public representations at the Perth conference (stating he "leads all strategic partnerships") and the email "you can proceed on the basis that I have full authority" give rise to a strong arguable case of ostensible (apparent) authority. Meridian was entitled to rely on these representations in negotiating the Heads of Agreement.
    26|- The constitution (requiring two directors or director + company secretary for execution) creates an internal limitation. However, third parties negotiating with a company are entitled to assume that internal restrictions have been complied with unless they have actual or constructive notice of the restriction (the Turquand rule and its limits). The key question is whether Meridian had constructive notice of Rohan's internal limitation — the facts indicate it did not.
    27|- DocuSign execution by Rohan alone may raise formal execution arguments. If the constitution requires multiple signatories for binding contracts, Arka may argue lack of formal execution; Meridian can respond that the company is estopped from denying Rohan's authority where it has represented otherwise.
    28|
    29|### Conclusion on Issue 1
    30|
    31|Meridian has a strong argument that Arka is bound by the Heads of Agreement on the basis of ostensible authority and the indoor-management/Turquand principles, subject to any narrow statutory/formal execution requirements under the Corporations Act or the constitution. The fact Meridian transferred funds and relied on Rohan's representations strengthens Meridian's position.
    32|
    33|## Analysis — Issue 2: Choice of law & enforcement
    34|
    35|### Rule
    36|- Choice of law clauses are generally upheld where parties have reasonable connection and the clause was freely agreed (See House of Lords authority and conflict of laws principles). Jurisdiction clauses may be severed or given effect.
    37|
    38|### Application
    39|- The choice of Western Australian law is valid on its face. Meridian can bring proceedings in Western Australia, and the clause supports enforcement there. However, enforcement in Australia of a contract with a foreign incorporated counterparty (Arka in India) may require local process (service, recognition) and may involve issues of public policy or foreign immunity — these are unlikely to be decisive here.
    40|
    41|### Conclusion on Issue 2
    42|
    43|Meridian can rely on the WA choice of law clause to litigate in Australia and to seek enforcement remedies, subject to cross-border enforcement mechanics.
    44|
    45|## Analysis — Issue 3: Remedies
    46|
    47|- Specific performance: equitable relief may be available for unique contracts, but courts are cautious for long-term commercial supply arrangements. Meridian’s remedy at law for breach (damages) is available; specific performance may be pursued if damages are an inadequate remedy.
    48|- Rectification: available where the written document fails to reflect prior agreement. Unlikely here unless a drafting mismatch is shown.
    49|- Estoppel: Arka may be estopped from denying Rohan’s authority where its conduct induced reliance.
    50|- Restitution: if Arka unjustly retains the $1.5m, Meridian can pursue restitution/quantum meruit.
    51|
    52|## Analysis — Issue 4: Claims against Rohan personally
    53|
    54|- Meridian may pursue claims against Rohan for misrepresentation and seek personal remedies if Rohan acted outside authority and misled Meridian. In addition, potential director’s duties breaches (s180–184 Corporations Act) could be relevant, and the company may have remedies against Rohan.

    56|## Recommendations
    57|
    58|1. Commence proceedings in WA seeking declarations of validity and enforcement, or urgent interlocutory relief to preserve funds.
    59|2. Preserve evidence: emails, conference materials, banking records, DocuSign records, witness statements.
    60|3. Consider seeking interlocutory injunctive relief to prevent Arka from dissipating funds or terminating access to technology.
    61|4. Evaluate alternate dispute resolution (negotiation/mediation) given cross-border corporate relationships.

    63|## Draft conclusion
    64|
    65|Meridian has a realistic prospect of enforcing the Heads of Agreement against Arka on the basis of apparent authority and related equitable principles. There are technical defences available to Arka (internal constitution non-compliance), but Meridian’s reliance on Rohan’s representations and payment of funds strengthens its position. Meridian should seek immediate preservation steps and consider commencing proceedings in WA while exploring pragmatic settlement.

## Statutory and authority citations (inline)

- Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) s127 (execution of documents): "A company may execute a document without using a common seal if the document is signed by:
  (a)  two directors of the company; or
  (b)  a director and a company secretary of the company; or
  (c)  for a proprietary company that has a sole director who is also the sole company secretary — that director."

- Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) s128 (effect of company instrument): provides that a company is bound by a document which appears to be duly executed by the company.

- Freeman & Lockyer v Buckhurst Park Properties (Mangal) Ltd [1964] 2 QB 480 — (Diplock LJ) outlines the test for ostensible authority where the company’s conduct represents that the agent has authority.

- Royal British Bank v Turquand (1856) 6 E & B 327 — the "indoor management" rule: persons dealing with a company are entitled to assume internal requirements have been complied with, subject to constructive notice.

- For director’s duties: Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) ss 180–184 (care & diligence; good faith; misuse of position and information); s588G (insolvent trading).

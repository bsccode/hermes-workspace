## Slide 10

Can members’ override the decisions of the board?

Members cannot usurp the powers of management from the board of directors
This was considered in Automatic Self Cleansing Filter Syndicate Co Ltd v Cunninghame [1906]
In this case, the internal governance rules conferred the powers to manage the company to the board of directors. One of the company’s shareholders arranged to sell the company’s assets and undertaking to another company.
He convened a meeting to pass a resolution directing the board of directors to enter into the sale agreement and affixing the common seal to that document.
The resolution was passed. However, the directors did not believe the sale was in the company’s best interest and refused to follow the resolution.
Question: Must the directors follow the directive by the members?
Held: No, the decision to sell the company’s assets and the undertaking was a management decision and was within the directors’ powers and not the members.
A similar decision was arrived in John Shaw & Sons (Salford) Ltd v Shaw [1935] – in this case, the board of directors resolved to bring action against certain directors to recoup debts owed to the company. The directors were also members who moved a resolution to withdraw the litigation. Held: the decision to commence litigation was within the powers of management, and members cannot override this decision.

## Slide 17

Development of Organic Theory

It originated from Lennard’s Carrying Co Ltd v Asiatic Petroleum Co Ltd (1915)
Facts: Lennard’s Carrying Co Ltd owned a ship. L was an active director of the company and took an active role in the ship’s management.
The ship carried oil owned by Asiatic Petroleum, and due to the ship’s unseaworthiness, it caught fire, and the cargo was destroyed.
Issue: Is the Company responsible. The argument was it was L who was responsible and not the Company
Held: L’s default was the default of the company 
Reason:  Company’s active and directing will must be imposed on the person of somebody who for some purposes may be called an agent, but who is the directing mind and will of the corporation the very ego of the corporation. That person may be under the direction of shareholders in general meetings; that person may be the board of directors itself.
Hence Mr L was the directing mind of the company then his action must unless a corporation is not to be liable at all, have been an action which was the action of the company itself.

## Slide 18

State of mind of the agent – state of mind of the company

HL Bolton (Engineering) Co Ltd v TJ Graham & Sons Ltd 
Denning LJ drew an analogy between a company and parts of a human body
It has a brain that controls what it does. It has hands which act in accordance with the directions from the centre
“A company may in many ways be likened to a human body. It has a brain and nerve centre which controls what it does. It also has hands which hold the tools and act in accordance with directions from the centre. Some of the people in the company are mere servants and agents who are nothing more than hands to do the work and cannot be said to represent the mind or will. Others are directors and managers who represent the directing mind and will of the company, and control what it does. The state of mind of these managers is the state of mind of the company and is treated by the law as such”

## Slide 19

Directors and Senior Management

These people are regarded as the directing mind and will of the company and their state of mind is that of the company
Tesco Supermarkets Ltd v Nattrass [1972]
Managers who are entrusted with significant degree of freedom from supervision of higher authority are those who are regarded as the directing mind and will of the company
Facts: Tesco owns a chain of supermarkets. In one of its stores a large advertisement was displayed which indicated that a particular item was on a reduced price. When the reduced item had all been sold, a shop assistant put out on display the same items marked at the normal prices. This was not reported to the store manager and the advertisement remained in the window.
A customer who saw the advertisement went in to buy the item on the advertised prices but was informed that it was not available for the advertised prices.
Company was sued for false or misleading advertisements. The company argued that it was the store manager’s fault not the company’s fault. 
Issue: was the store manager the directing mind of the company?
House of Lords held: Store manager lacked the necessary responsibility of control of company’s operations to be identified as the controlling mind of the company’s operations – he was NOT the controlling mind and will of the company.

## Slide 25

Contracting directly using a company seal

A company can enter into a contract directly by executing a contractual document. This involves directors or other authorised persons signing the document as an act of the company. In 195 Crown St Pty Ltd v Hoare [1969] 1 NSWR 193, Asprey JA explained that:
The execution of a document by a company ... resembles the execution of a document by a natural person who cannot write except through the medium of someone else who signs the disabled person’s name at his request and direction. An authorised signatory of a company’s document when acting under this section is the company’s amanuensis.
To bind a company to a transaction, the company must first authorise the transaction through the passing of a board of directors resolution to:
Enter into the transaction; and
Authorising the execution of the documents by the affixation of the company’s seal (if any) by officers authorised to do so.
If a company has a common seal, the document will be executed, and the seal affixed witnessed by :
2 directors / 1 director and 1 company secretary (s127(2))
If co has no common seal, the document will be executed by having the document signed:
2 directors. / 1dir and 1 company secretary (s127(1))

## Slide 26

Affixing Faulty Common Seal

If a company seal is affixed to a contract and the witnessing was not in accordance with the company’s constitution, the contract may still be binding on the company.
MYT Engineering Pty Ltd v Mulcon Pty Ltd [1999] HCA 24
The company seal was affixed to a deed of company arrangement and witnessed by one person even though the company’s constitution required the affixing of the seal to be witnessed by a director and countersigned by another director or secretary. It was held that the company validly executed the deed because the shareholders and members of the board authorised the director to do so and agreed that the instrument should be executed
However, affixing the seal may be void if the attesting directors or secretary acted without authority.

## Slide 31

Hely-Hutchinson v Brayhead Ltd

Facts: Richard was chairman of a company (the principal). The company’s board of directors allowed Richard to act as a managing director. To encourage Hely-Hutchinson ( also known as Lord Suirdale) to invest in a sister company, the managing director agreed to indemnify Lord Suirdale in case of any loss. After Lord Suirdale invested, the company went into liquidation. Lord Suirdale sought to recover the loss.
Issue: Could Hely-Hutchinson recover? Did Mr Richards have the authority to give the indemnity? 
Held: Richards did have authority; it was actual implied authority because the board had let Mr Richards continue to act and had, in fact, created actual authority.

## Slide 32

Apparent authority

Can arise even where the Principal has not in any way agreed that the agent could act on behalf of the principal
Requirements:
As stated in Freeman & Lockyer v Buckhurst Park Propertes (Mangal) Ltd [1964]
Principal holding out
Has actual authority to do so
Representation was relied upon
In Crabtree-Vickers Pty Ltd v Australian Direct Mail Advertising & Addressing Co Pty Ltd (1975) 33 CLR 72
The High Court applied the reasoning of Diplock LJ in Freeman and Lockyer and held that a person who merely has apparent authority is not capable of making representations for the company. The only persons who had actual authority to make the necessary representation. In this case, neither the three-man committee nor the full board (no evidence available) made any representations to the outsider. This decision can be criticised because it does not give due regard to commercial practice. It is almost impossible for an outsider in the position of Crabtree-Vickers to discover who has actual authority to make representations for the company.
Holding Out – representation is made to the outside contracting party that the agent has authority to enter on behalf of the company into the contract – the agent is ‘held out’
Actual authority – representation must be by the company or someone with actual authority – Pacific Carriers Limited v BNP Paribas Ltd [2004]
Reliance – outsiders must rely on this representation

## Slide 33

Freeman & Lockyer v Buckhurst Park Properties

Mr Freeman and Mr Lockyer sued Buckhurst Park Ltd and its director, Kapoor, for unpaid fees for their architecture work on developing the ‘Buckhurst Park Estate’. Initially, the company planned to buy and resell the land, but that fell through. Kapoor had acted alone (as if he were a managing director) in engaging the architects without proper authority. The company argued the agreement did not bind it.
Held: ‘An ‘apparent’ or ‘ostensible’ authority . . is a legal relationship between the principal and the contractor created by a representation, made by the principal to the contractor, intended to be and acted upon by the contractor, that the agent has authority to enter on behalf of the principal into a contract of a kind within the scope of the ‘apparent’ authority, to render the principal liable to perform any obligations imposed upon him by such contract.

## Slide 35

Indoor management rule

A person dealing with the company in good faith may assume that acts were within its constitution and powers have been properly and duly performed and are not bound to inquire whether the acts of internal governance were regular
The outsider has the right to assume that e.g. the directors were properly appointed; all approval to enter into agreements were rightly procured – Royal British Bank v Turquand
When is the right to assume is lost?
If third party actually knew that agent did not have the authority; or
Where the third party was put on inquiry but fails to inquire – Northside Developments Pty Ltd v Registrar General

Northside Developments Pty Ltd v Registrar General

Facts: 
In the Northside case, a bank loan was signed by Mr Sturgess, a director and his son who purported to sign as the company secretary. They then borrowed money from the bank by mortgaging the company’s major asset. This money was used for their purposes.  The other directors were not aware of this.

## Slide 38

Story v Advance Bank Australia Ltd

Facts: 

Mr & Mrs Story were directors and shareholders of Fleetwood Star Pty Ltd. Mr Story obtained a $1m loan from Advance Bank by forging Mrs Story’s signature and secured the loan on the company’s property. The loan was used for the company’s business and their household expenses.
Issue: Was Fleetwood Star Pty Ltd bound? Was the bank put upon inquiry?
Held: Fleetwood Star Pty Ltd was bound. The bank was not put upon inquiry as part of the money was used for the company’s business.

## Slide 40

S129(1) Constitutions and replaceable rule

Person dealing with a   company may assume that the company’s constitution (if any) and any provisions of the Corporations Act that apply as replaceable rules have been complied with.
Section 129(1) does not require the person to know the constitution or replaceable rules. 
In  Australia and New Zealand Banking Group Limited v Frenmast Pty limited [2013] NSWCA 459, it was held that for there to be dealings by an outsider with a company, the person with whom the outsider has the transactions must have actual or apparent authority to undertake some negotiation. 
Each section 129 assumption is separate and discrete - Brick and Pipe Industries Ltd v Occidental Life Nominees Pty Limited. However, the outsider may rely upon more than one assumption -Bank of New Zealand v Fiberi Pty Limited

## Slide 42

S129(3) Person held out is officer or agent

Refers to Officers and being held out. Third party can assume that anyone who is ‘held out’ by the company to be its officer or agent has been duly appointed: S129(3)(a)
The person who holds out someone as an agent or officer of the company has ACTUAL authority.
In Freeman v Lockyear, the board of directors who have the   actual   authority   held   out   agent   as   managing director.
In Brick   and   Pipe   v   Occidental   Life,   the Managing director who had actual   authority impliedly held out the agent as company secretary; the other agent who only had apparent authority DID NOT have the power to hold out the agent as company secretary.   
S129(3)(b) requires the   authority   to   exercise   powers   and perform duties are customarily exercised or performed by that kind of officer or agent. “What can an officer or agent of that kind in a similar company normally be able to do?”

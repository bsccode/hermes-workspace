## Slide 5

As replaceable rules are dispersed across the Corporations Act, it can be identified where replaceable rules are stated in the heading.
, E.g. CORPORATIONS ACT 2001 – SECT 198A [Powers of directors (replaceable rule—see section 135)]
Replaceable rule, as the word suggests, can be replaced by the company should it choose to do so. Most RR applies to both Proprietary and Public Companies
The RR is subject to any provisions contained in a shareholder joint venture agreement – Easton Mining Pty Ltd v Eastone Holding Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 1850
Some RR may only apply to proprietary companies
Whereas, for Public companies, these may be mandatory rules, e.g. s203C and S203D
S135(3) failure to comply with RR is not a contravention of the Corporations Act 2001
Contravention of RR will not give the party affected the right to apply for a statutory injunction.

## Slide 11

HOW CAN a company amend or repeal its constitution?

If a company has adopted its own constitution it may amend, modify or repeal its constitution following the requirements stated under s136(2)
A special resolution must be passed in the general meeting (75%) and if there are additional requirements as stated under the constitution – then these requirements must also be complied with s136(3) and such requirement cannot be altered unless provided by the constitution s136(4) [entrenching provision].
If it is a public company, then the special resolution must be lodged with ASIC within 14 days after it is passed.

## Slide 16

Effect of Constitution and Replaceable Rules

It is deemed to be a statutory contract
S140(1) states it is a contract between:
Company and each member  - s140(1)(a)
Company and each director – s140(1)(b)
Member and member s140(1)(c)
Construction of contracts generally takes into account the understanding of what a reasonable person would have taken the contract to mean
A company may modify or repeal its constitution or provision of the constitution by passing a special resolution (s136(2))
S140(2) – a member is not bound by a modification of the constitution after becoming  a member so far as the modification requires the member to take up additional shares; or increases the members liability to contribute to the share capital of, or otherwise to pay money to, the company; or imposes or increases a restriction on the right to transfer the shares already held by the member.
A member is only bound by such modification where this has been agreed in writing.

## Slide 17

Contract between company and members

It is a contract between the company and members
Company can take action against members to seek compliance of the constitution / RR
Hickman v Kent or Romney Marsh Sheep-Breeders Assoc (1915)
Held: That member must follow the terms of the constitution, which was to refer any matter to arbitration before commencing legal proceedings.
Only provisions that confers rights on members in their capacity as members are enforceable as a contract. Hence not all provisions have contractual effect.

## Slide 20

Contract between members

S140(1)(c) provides for a contract existing between each member of the company
This can apply to the situation of pre-emptive rights provision, which gives shareholders rights of first refusal to buy other shareholders’ shares or to sell their shares to the remaining shareholders.
Re Caratti Holding Co Pty Ltd [1975]
One of the clauses in the company’s constitution gave powers to the majority members to compulsorily acquire the shares of the minority shareholder. 
Held:
There was a contractual right (s140(1)(c)) to enforce the compulsory acquisition. The clause can be enforced by the company or the majority shareholders.[The acquisition in the case, however, was disallowed because it was oppressive]
See also Gibbins Investments Pty Ltd v Savage [2011] – arrangement to transfer shares in contravention to pre-emptive rights was challenged successfully

## Slide 21

CONTRACT BETWEEN COMPANY AND ITS DIRECTORS AND SECRETARIES

S140(1)(b) this may deal with the appointment, powers and remuneration of directors
It is common for directors to enter into separate employment contracts of service that is independent of the company’s constitution.
Shuttleworth v Cox Bros & Co (Maidenhead) Ltd [1927]
Company’s constitution had a provision which allowed a person to be appointed a director for life. Grounds of removal were stated. The company decided to alter the constitution and include additional grounds of removal which included a request in writing and executed by all the non affected directors of the removal of one of them. Such a request was passed and a director was removed. 
Court rejected the argument that clause which appointed a director for life was a contract which cannot be varied without the concerned directors consent. It was held that the clause appointing him for life was subject to the statutory power given to the company to alter its constitution. In this case the director had no separate contract independent of the constitution.

## Slide 22

FAILURE TO COMPLY

A failure to comply with the replaceable rules is not a contravention of the Act
It represents a private obligation – can seek for a declaration.
A person seeking compliance through an injunction must do so under the ordinary jurisdiction of the Supreme Court rather than s1324 under the Corporations Act. Normally injunction and specific performance is not granted to enforce employment contracts on unwilling parties.
Non-compliance with internal governance rules may amount to a procedural irregularity – which mean the action is not invalidated unless the court is of the opinion that the irregularity has caused or may cause substantial injustice.

## Slide 27

Duties of a promoter

A promoter must make full disclosure of their interest in any contracts that may be entered into on behalf of the company.
Non-disclosure will be a breach of this duty
Erlarger v New Sombrero Phosphate Co (1878)
 Facts: A syndicate purchased an island in the hope it will contain valuable minerals. A company was formed with the purpose of purchasing the island from the syndicate. The head of the syndicate nominated the directors of the company who in turn ratified the contract. It was found that the island was worth lesser than the amount it was sold for, which resulted in the company making a loss and the promoters making a profit.
Held: company can rescind the contract with the purchase money returned to the company and the island to the syndicate. 
Rationale: Promoters were under a duty to ensure that the board of directors were independent and full disclosure of their interests were to be made, which would have allowed an independent assessment of the transaction.

## Slide 30

COMMON LAW POSITION
Prior to registration a company does not exist and therefore a company cannot enter into a binding contract
Hence a person cannot make legally binding contracts in the name of the company in the anticipation that the company will be registered.
A company is also not bound by a pre-registration contract entered on behalf of it prior to its registration. This is premised on the argument that a company cannot have an agent before it is registered because the law of agency requires the presence of a principal at the time when the agent enters into the transaction.
It was also added that the person who entered into the pre-registration contract may not also be personally bound by the contract – Black v Smallwood (1966)
A person entering into a contract on behalf of a company yet to be registered will only be personally bound if that person intended to contract as the principal: Kelner v Baxter. Further a company cannot ratify the contract after it has been formed.
The only way a company can be liable for a pre-registration contract under common law, was to enter into a fresh new contract upon registration.

## Slide 31

Difference between kelner v baxter and black v smallwood

Kelner v Baxter
At the time of contracting both parties were aware that the company was not formed. In such case, personal liability will be triggered as long a binding contract was intended at the time of the contract and all parties knew the company was not formed yet.
Black v Smallwood
In this case there was a mistaken believe that the company was in existence. Hence, the persons who were signing the contract were of the belief that they were signing on behalf of the company and therefore there was no intention on the parties to be personally liable.

## Slide 32

CORPORATIONS ACT 2001 (CTH)
The anomalies raised by the common law was rectified by s131 which replaces the law in relation to pre-registration contracts
S131 (1) – a company will be responsible for a pre-registration contract if:
the company is formed; and
company ratifies the contract within the agreed time or reasonable time
S131(2) – if company is not registered or if registered does not ratify the contract then the person who entered the contract will be liable.
Bay v Illawara Stationery Supplies Pty Ltd
Accountant and four promoters entered into a pre-registration contract for stationery supplies. Company was subsequently formed but it failed to ratify the contract as a result the supplier sued all of the promoters. Court held only accountant liable as he was the one who signed the contract. But the accountant may claim from the other promoters.

S131(4) if company ratifies the contract and does not fulfil its obligations, the court may order the person to pay all or part of the damages
S132(1) a person who enter into a pre-registration contract can exempt liability for damages if the contracting party signs a release of liability

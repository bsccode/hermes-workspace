# Question 3 — Copyright Advice (Draft)

Issue

Whether Clare’s “visual elixir effect” (the gold-dusted cold brew packaging/display effect described as "dancing in the moonlight") is protectable by copyright.

Answer (short)

Copyright protection will apply only if the visual elixir effect qualifies as an "artistic work" (or falls within another recognised category) and has sufficient fixation and originality. If Clare's effect arises from artistic choices in packaging/label, photographs, or designed visual presentation, those particular expressions (labels, photos, packaging artworks) are likely protected, but the underlying idea (a sparkling visual effect) and functional packaging features are not.

IRAC — Reasoning and explanation

Issue

Is the visual elixir effect a protectable expression (rather than an idea) that is in a material form and original? Is any aspect of the product or marketing likely to meet the subsistence requirements for copyright?

Rule

Key authorities and rules:
- Copyright subsists in original literary, dramatic, musical or artistic works: s32 (Australian Copyright Act 1968) as cited in lecture slides (Module IP Part 1).
- Subsistence requires fixation in a material form (s10(1), s22) and originality (independent intellectual effort) — see slides and cases (e.g., Chapman Kelly v Chicago Park District for fixation discussion; Corelli v Gray on independent creation principle).
- Copyright protects expression, not ideas (TRIPS Art. 9(2); Donoghue v Allied Newspapers Ltd examples in slides).

Application

- Fixation / material form: If the visual elixir effect exists only as a live in-store display (e.g., light and glitter in the cup when shaken), it may be transient and risk failing the fixation/permanence criteria. However, if the effect is embodied in packaging artwork, photography, label design, or recorded video advertising, those fixed expressions will be protected.
- Originality: Packaging design or a bespoke label that creates the visual effect will usually have sufficient originality (creative choices in composition, colour, typography) to attract protection.
- Idea vs expression: The general idea of a sparkling gold-dusted beverage is an idea and not protected; however, Clare’s specific artistic implementation (photographs, bottle design, label artwork) is protected.

Conclusion

Advise Clare that:
- She likely has copyright in any fixed artworks: product photographs, label/packaging artistic works, advertisements and any recorded performances that capture the effect.
- The ephemeral in-glass shimmering effect itself, if not reduced to a material/fixed medium (e.g., photos, videos), may be hard to protect by copyright alone.
- Recommended practical steps: register or create dated copies of product artwork, retain master Photoshop/Illustrator files, register/record video adverts, and consider supplementary protection via design registration (Designs Act) or trade dress/trademark.

Citations

- Copyright Act 1968 (Cth) — see s 31–32 (subsistence of copyright in "original" works) and s 10(1) on "material form" (fixation).
- TRIPS, Article 9(2)

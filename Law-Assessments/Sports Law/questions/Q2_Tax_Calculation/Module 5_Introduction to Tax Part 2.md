## Slide 18

ORDINARY INCOME

Section 6-5 does NOT state what is ordinary income. But case law mentioned that:
Income is something which actually comes in and not merely the increase in value of existing assets.
Income receipts may be differentiated from receipts of a capital nature as they are received regularly or periodically. (Examples are salary, dividends and interest and pensions which are received regularly.)
This is income according to ordinary concepts, i.e. a taxpayer’s normal income-earning activities, e.g. wages and salaries, directors’ fees, interest, rental income, business income.
Income under s.6-5 is something paid in money or that can be converted to money i.e. money or money’s worth.
The return received on capital invested is considered to be income – e.g. dividends paid on shares, interest on funds invested and rent from properties.  The shares, money lent and property are capital assets.

## Slide 19

ILLEGAL INCOME

The fact that income may come from illegal means (e.g. drug-dealing, prostitution, bookmaking, insider trading or theft) is irrelevant, it is still classified as income and must be declared for taxation purposes.

## Slide 20

STATUTORY INCOME

Legislation specifically makes certain items of income assessable. These items are known as statutory income
Some examples are franking credits under s.207-20(1), allowances under s.15-2 and capital gains.
We will examine the statutory income provisions in due course

## Slide 31

GENERAL DEDUCTION - THE FIRST AND SECOND POSITIVE LIMBS

Look at the loss or outgoing and see if it can satisfy one or both of the first two positive limbs of s 8-1. 
a. Under the first limb, the loss or outgoing MUST be incurred in gaining or producing assessable income whereas 
b. Under the 2nd positive limb it is the business which must be carried on for the purpose of gaining or producing the assessable income and the loss or outgoing MUST be NECESSARILY incurred in carrying on that business. 
Timing of an expense is important because it governs the tax year in which a deduction can be claimed.
NOTE: Deductions must be shown in whole dollars.

## Slide 32

CLOTHING AND UNIFORM

Most clothing is termed “conventional clothing” and is a private expense.  Accordingly, the cost of buying and maintaining such clothing cannot be deducted.
Occupational Specific clothing is deductible, e.g. chef uniform.
Compulsory uniforms are deductible, e.g. QANTAS attendants.
Non-compulsory uniforms with employers name embossed are deductible under Div 34 if registered on the Register of Approved Occupational Clothing  Register.
See ATO booklet on Clothing and laundry  https://www.ato.gov.au/individuals-and-families/income-deductions-offsets-and-records/deductions-you-can-claim/clothes-and-items-you-wear-at-work/clothing-laundry-and-dry-cleaning-expenses

## Slide 10

WHAT IS ASSESSABLE INCOME?

Assessable income is made up of:
Ordinary Income – the meaning of ordinary income is based on common law. There is no statutory definition.
Statutory Income –  amounts that are specifically made income by statutes 
But it excludes
Exempt Income - income that is specifically excluded as taxable income via the provisions of 97 ITAA and 36 ITAA
Income which is neither assessable income nor exempt income

DEDUCTIONS

Deductions are being any loss or outgoing to the extent that it is incurred in gaining or producing assessable income or is necessarily incurred in carrying on a business for the purposes of gaining or producing assessable income

## Slide 14

WHAT ARE TAX OFFSETS?

Tax Offsets (credits and rebates) are designed to reduce the amount of pay taxable by a taxpayer and include: 
Low-income tax offset (LITO)
Franking Credits (related to dividends)
Small Business Income Tax Offset (SBITO)
Pay-as-You-Go (PAYG) instalments/withholding

## Slide 15

WHAT IS PAYG?

The PAYG regime is designed so that the majority of taxpayers pre-pay their annual tax liability BY THEIR EMPLOYER throughout the year, as the income is earned, rather than making one large payment of tax at the end of a financial year.

## Slide 17

MEDICARE LEVY

Introduced in 1984 to provide Australian residents with access to affordable health care.
It allows for both free and subsidized treatment by medical practitioners or specialists and free treatment as a public patient in a public hospital.
Individual resident taxpayers are liable to pay a Medicare levy if their taxable income exceeds the relevant threshold.  (For 2022/2023year - $24,277 to $30,345)
The current Medicare levy is 2% and is applied to the taxpayer’s taxable income (Assessable Income less Allowable Deductions).  
Example 1.3
Ellie had a taxable income of $100,000. Her Medicare levy payable:
2% x 100,000 = $2000.00

## Slide 18

MEDICARE LEVY

The Medicare levy may be reduced for certain low-income earners.  
Medicare is not payable if an individual taxpayer’s taxable income does not exceed $24,276.  
Special rates apply in certain situations for example married couples, families, senior Australians and pensioners.

## Slide 19

MEDICARE LEVY SURCHARGE

A 1% to 1.5% Medicare levy surcharge is imposed if:
income exceeds the imposed thresholds; and 
no private health hospital cover. 
The threshold since 1 July 2015 has been $90,000 for single and $180,000 for a couple or family $180,000 plus $1,500 for each dependent child after the first. Please check ATO website for the updated threshold
Students will NOT be examined on Medicare levy surcharge

## Slide 20

LOW-INCOME TAX OFFSET (LITO)

Individual taxpayers who have a taxable income less than $37,500 are entitled to an offset of $700.  This phases out till the offset cuts out at a taxable income of $66,667.  Note, this offset is calculated in dollars and cents.

## Slide 25

ORDINARY INCOME

Section 6-5 does NOT state what is ordinary income. But case law mentioned that:
Income is something which actually comes in and not merely the increase in value of existing assets.
Income receipts may be differentiated from receipts of a capital nature as they are received regularly or periodically. (Examples are salary, dividends and interest and pensions which are received regularly.)
This is income according to ordinary concepts, i.e. a taxpayer’s normal income-earning activities, e.g. wages and salaries, directors’ fees, interest, rental income, business income.
Income under s.6-5 is something paid in money or that can be converted to money i.e. money or money’s worth.
The return received on capital invested is considered to be income – e.g. dividends paid on shares, interest on funds invested and rent from properties.  The shares, money lent and property are capital assets.


---

## Slide 26

CAPITAL RECEIPTS

Capital Receipts are not income under s6-5. Capital receipts can be:
proceeds from the sale of capital assets
compensation for the loss of a capital right to income
gifts, and windfall gains from lottery or betting wins. 
Capital Gains Tax may apply to capital receipts


---

## Slide 27

INCOME VS CAPITAL

A useful analogy is that of the tree and its fruit from Eisner v Macomber (1920) 252 US 189.  
Sale of Tree – Capital v Sale of Fruit – Income 
Sale of Shares – Capital v Dividends – Income 
Similarly, expenditure can be on capital account (e.g. buying the tree) while other expenses are on revenue account (e.g. fertilising and watering the tree.)

## Slide 28

STATUTORY INCOME

Legislation specifically makes certain items of income assessable. These items are known as statutory income
Some examples are franking credits under s.207-20(1), allowances under s.15-2 and capital gains.
We will examine the statutory income provisions in due course

## Slide 29

DIVIDENDS

Dividends represent income from property and are regarded as income according to ordinary concepts.
A company as a separate legal entity pays tax on company profits. A company can distribute dividends to its shareholders from taxed profits referred to as “franked” dividends and those from untaxed profits are referred to as “unfranked” dividends.   (See example of dividend statement on page 66 of textbook)
All dividends ARE assessable income under s.44.  Additionally, where a shareholder receives a franked dividend, franking credits ARE included in assessable income. Then taxed at shareholder’s marginal rate.

## Slide 30

ILLEGAL INCOME

The fact that income may come from illegal means (e.g. drug-dealing, prostitution, bookmaking, insider trading or theft) is irrelevant, it is still classified as income and must be declared for taxation purposes.


---

## Slide 31

CARRYING ON A BUSINESS

Is a person carrying on a business, or is it a hobby? Commissioner will look to the following criteria to decide:
Profitability.  Is there an expectation of a profit?
Size. Generally, the larger the size of the operations the more likely it is that the Commissioner would consider a business was being carried on.  Sometimes very small operations have been considered to be a business.
Repetition and regularity of activities.  However, in some instances a single activity has been held to constitute the carrying on of a business.
The keeping of records and the running of the operations in a business-like manner likely to indicate that this would be a business.

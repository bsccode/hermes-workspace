# Question 1 — Advice on suitable business structure (Draft)

Issue

What business structure is most suitable for Heather’s planned expansion of her cultured-meat-for-pets business given her circumstances (high personal wealth, existing rental properties, academic employment, desire for good cash-flow and concern about risk/unsuccessful exploration)?

Answer (short)

A proprietary limited company (Pty Ltd) is the primary recommendation for Heather’s operating vehicle, combined with a discretionary family trust (or unit trust) holding the company shares for asset protection and tax-streaming flexibility. This combination balances limited liability, investor-friendly capital-raising, corporate continuity and tax planning, while the trust can protect personal assets and give distribution flexibility.

IRAC — Reasoning and explanation

Issue

Heather needs a structure that:
- limits personal liability for business risks (cultured-meat development and possible unsuccessful projects);
- supports raising capital or bringing on investors without exposing her personal estate;
- preserves cash flow (tax and distribution flexibility);
- provides reasonable administrative overhead given her resources.

Rule

Key legal rules and authorities:
- Separate legal personality and limited liability available via a company (Corporations Act 2001 (Cth); common-law principle exemplified in Salomon v A Salomon & Co Ltd [1897] AC 22).
- Tax rules: company profits are taxed at company rates and dividends may be franked; trusts provide a vehicle for streaming income to beneficiaries (Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 (Cth) — franking credit regime; trustee taxation rules).
- Partnership and sole trader forms expose owners to unlimited personal liability (Partnership Act and general common law principles).

Application

Compare options:

1) Sole trader
- Pros: Simple, low admin, full control.
- Cons: No liability protection — Heather’s substantial personal wealth (and rental properties) would be at risk if the business failed or was sued. Not recommended given her exposure.

2) Partnership
- Pros: Simple for multiple active partners.
- Cons: Joint and several liability for partners; unsuitable if Heather wants to ring-fence risk and protect personal assets.

3) Company (Pty Ltd)
- Pros: Limited liability for shareholders; separate legal entity (Salomon); easier to raise capital from investors; corporate continuity; clear corporate governance and familiarity for investors; profits can be retained to preserve cash flow.
- Cons: Administrative compliance (ASIC, financial statements, directors’ duties under Corporations Act 2001 (Cth)); potential double layering of tax (company tax then dividend tax), although franking credits mitigate double taxation when profits distributed.
- Application to Heather: a Pty Ltd operating company limits her personal liability and is investor-friendly — fits her capital and risk profile.

4) Trust (discretionary/family trust) holding company shares
- Pros: Asset protection and income distribution flexibility (trustee holds shares in company; trustee can distribute profits to beneficiaries in tax-efficient ways); shields personal assets from direct business creditors if properly structured.
- Cons: Setup and ongoing trustee obligations; must be run at arm’s length and with proper formalities.
- Application to Heather: a discretionary trust holding the Pty Ltd shares is common for high-net-worth small businesses — it protects assets and allows income streaming to family members in low tax brackets (subject to trust law and anti-avoidance rules).

Recommendation (practical)

- Primary vehicle: establish a proprietary limited company (Pty Ltd) as the operating entity for the cultured-meat business.
- Holding structure: use a discretionary (family) trust as the shareholder of the company (trustee as legal owner, Heather + family members as potential beneficiaries) to provide flexibility in distributions and some asset protection.
- Retain personal assets (rental properties) outside the operating company; consider separate holding companies or trusts for property to ring-fence liabilities.
- Governance: appoint experienced non-executive director or company secretary for compliance; maintain proper minutes, accounts and ASIC filings.

Caveats and practical steps

- Directors’ duties: Heather (if a director) will owe duties under the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) — in particular s 180 (care and diligence), s 181 (good faith and proper purpose), s 182 (improper use of position), s 183 (improper use of information) and s 588G (insolvent trading). See Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) ss 180-184; s 588G.
- Insolvency tipping point: if the company becomes insolvent, directors can be personally liable for insolvent trading (s588G Corporations Act) — maintain cash-flow forecasting and creditor management.
- Tax/Trust rules: implement distributions carefully and obtain tax advice to avoid negative tax outcomes or breaches of trust law.

Primary references / citations

- Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) — company formation, directors’ duties and governance obligations.
- Salomon v A Salomon & Co Ltd [1897] AC 22 (separate legal personality).
- Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 (Cth) — franking credits and trustee tax provisions (see Division relating to trusts and franking in the Act).

Next steps (to implement)

2. Incorporate Pty Ltd and register ABN/TFN, open corporate bank account.
3. Appoint directors and set up basic corporate governance (minutes, registers).
4. Obtain specialist tax and trust advice to finalise the trust deed and distribution strategy.

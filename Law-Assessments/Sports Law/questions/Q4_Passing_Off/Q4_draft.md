# Question 4 — Passing Off (Draft)

Issue

Whether Clare can sue Jimmy for passing off in relation to his product "Jimmy Moonlight" which mimics Clare’s Victory Cold Brew (less gold dust, sells for $5/litre vs $1,900/litre) and has gained large online popularity.

Answer (short)

Clare may have a viable passing off action if she can establish (1) reputation (goodwill) in the relevant market, (2) misrepresentation by Jimmy leading to consumer confusion, and (3) damage (or a real likelihood of damage) to Clare's goodwill. The factual matrix (price difference, marketing channels, exclusivity) will be critical and may make success uncertain — courts focus on whether consumers are likely to be misled about origin or endorsement.

IRAC — Reasoning and explanation

Rule

Elements of passing off (common law):
1. Goodwill or reputation attached to the claimant’s goods or services in the relevant market.
2. Misrepresentation by the defendant that leads, or is likely to lead, the public to believe the defendant’s goods are those of the claimant (or are connected).
3. Damage to the claimant’s goodwill as a result (or likelihood of damage).

Leading authorities:
- Erven Warnink BV v J Townend & Sons Ltd [1979] AC 731 (Anglo-Holland) — sets out elements and approach to passing off in the UK context.
- Australian jurisprudence applying passing off principles (see Australian Woollen Mills v F S Walton & Co; also see recent authorities applying the same framework).
- Statutory alternatives: Trade Marks Act 1995 (Cth) for registered trade mark infringement; Australian Consumer Law (s 18, Competition and Consumer Act 2010) for misleading or deceptive conduct.

Application

1. Goodwill / reputation
- Clare’s brand is exclusive, invitation-only, and targeted to celebrity clients. This niche market can still confer goodwill if Clare can produce evidence (sales, invitations, press coverage, social media testimony).

2. Misrepresentation
- Key question: would an ordinary consumer (or Clare’s target consumer) be likely to confuse Jimmy’s product with Victory Cold Brew or believe it is associated with Clare? The large price differential and different distribution channels (exclusive invite-only vs mass social media) may reduce the likelihood of confusion. However, if Jimmy copies distinctive aspects of Clare’s branding (name, packaging, labels, trade dress, or slogans), that could constitute sufficient misrepresentation.

3. Damage
- If Jimmy’s product causes dilution of Clare’s exclusivity, tarnishes her reputation, or causes consumers to associate inferior quality with her brand, Clare can show damage. Even absent actual loss, a real and substantial likelihood of damage can suffice.

Practical considerations

- Evidence gathering: obtain screenshots of Jimmy’s marketing, compare packaging/labels, collect sales/press coverage showing Clare’s market presence, and gather consumer testimony indicating confusion.
- Alternative or complementary claims: consider registered trademark infringement (if Clare has a TM), passing off plus ACL s 18 claims (misleading/deceptive conduct), and design right or copyright claims if Jimmy has copied specific artworks or photographs.

Conclusion

Clare has arguable grounds for passing off but success depends heavily on evidence of confusion and the similarity of the branding/packaging. Given the factual weaknesses (price and positioning differences), consider sending a cease-and-desist and pursuing alternative IP remedies (trademark, design registration, ACL) alongside litigation as appropriate.

- Trade Marks Act 1995 (Cth)

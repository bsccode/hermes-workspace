## Slide 4

A brief history of Equity

In 1066, the Normans conquered England.
The Norman Kings imposed their authority on their (largely Anglo Saxon) subjects.
Norman Kings would hear and determine cases brought before them. Their guiding principle was, no matter the place where the case was determined, like cases would be decided in like manner. 
Hence, there would be one law common to all England. This is the origin of the term ‘common law’.
Norman Kings eventually delegated the job of hearing and determining cases. This was the origin of the common law courts.

However, over time common law litigation became overly technical and rigid.
In the 15th century, English subjects again approached the King directly to resolve disputes.
The King delegated these disputes to his Chancellor.
Chancellors at this time were priests, and determined disputes not according to common law precedent but to Christian precepts. The focus was on conscience.
Eventually, the Court of Chancey was established to exercise the Chancellor’s equitable jurisdiction.
Common law and Equity work alongside each other.

## Slide 8

An example of a legal interest in land is an interest in land registered under the Transfer of Land Act 1893 (WA) (‘TLA’).
So when you buy that 2 bedroom, 2 bathroom (or 2 bedroom, 4 bathroom) dream home and become registered as the owner at Landgate that is a legal interest in property (about as legal as you can get!). 
However, what about just after you have signed the contract of sale but before you are registered at Landgate? 
In that case, as we will see, you have an equitable interest (by virtue of a trust implied at law).

## Slide 23

A conflict exists between s 4 of the Statute of Frauds and s 34(1)(a) Property Law Act (the latter seeming to suggest an interest in land be in writing and not just evidenced by it as required by the former).
Marist Brothers Community Inc v Shire of Harvey (1994) 14 WAR 69 – this leading case reflects the current position that the formalities required for contracts for the sale of land ie s 34(1)(a) PLA does not apply to contracts for the sale of an interest in land since it is directed to the creation of interests in land by conveyance or other instruments such as express trusts and not to agreements as such. I do not consider it was being said that s34 extended to ordinary contracts of sale of the fee simple, which would be transferred by a Transfer of Land Act transfer when the time for settlement arrived. The agreement that has been made enforceable is by reason of the writing satisfying the Statute of Frauds. In these circumstances it is open  to obtain an order for specific performance by effecting the conveyance.

## Slide 26

The effect of the Statute of Frauds:
A contract for the sale of an interest in land must be evidenced in writing. (Note: this is not the same as saying the contract must be in written form like s 34 PLA – only that there is something in writing to evidence the making of the contract). 
A defective contract purporting to create an interest in land (eg wholly oral or no sufficient memorandum in writing) is not invalid but it is unenforceable.
AND 
An enforceable contract will vest an equitable interest in the land to the purchaser. Lysaght v Edwards (1876) 2 Ch D 499:
It appears to me that the effect of a contract of sale has been settled for more than two centuries … [T]he moment you have a valid contract of sale, the vendor becomes in equity a trustee for the purchaser of the estate sold, and the beneficial ownership passes to the purchaser.
A legal interest will follow once all the formalities pertaining to land title –registration at Landgate for Torrens system land, or registering a deed for old system land, are met (see previous slides).

## Slide 27

Bunny Industries Ltd v FSW Enterprises [1982] Qd R 712
Facts: (The classic land sale screw-over). P contracted to purchase land from D1. Later D1 contracted to sell land to D2 and executed transfer of land to D2. P sought declaration (equitable remedy) that D1 held land on trust for P following contract to buy land between P and D1.
Held: D1 held land in trust for P and breach of trust occurred. Some principles outlined in case:
1. 	On execution of the contract, vendor becomes trustee for purchaser (but not a bare trustee – yet)
2. 	Purchaser may devise, alienate and charge their equitable interest, so it is not merely a right in contract.
3. 	The extent of the equitable interest is based on the amount of purchase monies paid. (Here, the vendor progresses towards becoming a bare trustee, which occurs upon payment of 100% purchase sum).
Where there a clear and undisputed contract, the court will not allow vendor to transfer legal estate to a third person. (Hence, a purchaser can lodge a caveat to protect interest.)

## Slide 28

Bunny Industries Ltd v FSW Enterprises [1982] Qd R 712
The Court applied the well known principle in Lysaght v Edwards (1876) 2 Ch D 499: A specifically enforceable contract of sale confers an equitable interest on the purchaser of the land. Equity deems as done that which ought to be done (maxim).
The principle in this case applies to both general law land (very rare as we will see in a later lecture) and Torrens system land.
Note that all formalities of contract for the sale of land must have been adhered to (ie s 4 Statute of Frauds or part performance).

## Slide 29

The principle that a specifically enforceable contract of sale confers an equitable interest on the purchaser of the fee simple estate in land applies also to enforceable contracts for the grant of a lease (even if the legal requirements are not satisfied).
Leading case: Walsh v Lonsdale (1882) 21 Ch D 9.
Facts: Lonsdale entered into a written agreement for grant of a lease of a weaving shed and other machinery to Walsh for 7 years. Walsh was to put the shed into working order, and pay rent yearly in advance However, no deed was executed for this as required. Walsh went into possession of shed, used his own steam power for running machinery and paid rent quarterly. Lonsdale sought immediate payment of yearly rent in advance and put in a distress claim (landlord’s right to sell tenant’s goods to cover arrears of rent, which is now no longer legal).
Held: Equity prevailed in this case. Lonsdale had the right to distress. Walsh had to pay a year in advance as if the lease had been in a signed document (ie a legal lease). Equity deems as done that which ought to be done.

## Slide 31

The elements of the doctrine of part performance:
The parties must have arrived at a concluded agreement.
The plaintiff on faith of the agreement, must have performed acts that were either required by the agreement or else both permitted by it or encouraged by the defendant; and in consequence, the plaintiff must have altered his position to such an extent that it would be inequitable to allow the defendant to plead that the contract was not evidenced in writing.
The acts performed must point plainly and not merely in an equivocal fashion to the existence of an agreement between the parties.
Circumstances must be such that were contract in writing to exist, equity would decree specific performance.
See McBride v Sandland (1918) 25 CLR 69

## Slide 33

The 5 requirements for specific performance:
The contract must be binding, either because it is in writing, or because of a sufficient act of part performance.
The obligation to transfer property must be unconditional and not subject to the approval of a third party.
The vendor must be able to give good title, or the purchaser must be prepared to accept title defects.
The contract must not be unconscionable or inequitable.
The purchaser must not have breached any essential term.

## Slide 35

The plaintiff assumed that a particular legal relationship existed between the plaintiff and the defendant or expected that a particular legal relationship would exist between them and that the defendant would not be free to withdraw from the expected legal relationship.
The defendant has induced the plaintiff to adopt that assumption or expectation.
The plaintiff acts or abstains from acting in reliance on the assumption or expectation.
The defendant knew or intended him/her to do so.
The plaintiff’s action or inaction will occasion detriment to him/her if the assumption or expectation is not fulfilled.
The defendant has failed to act to avoid that detriment to the plaintiff.

## Slide 37

Property can also be fragmented from a jurisdiction perspective into equitable and legal interests.
Equitable interests can come about in real property by purchasing a property pursuant to a contract of sale– the vendor holds the property on trust for the purchaser.
Acquiring an equitable interest by contract in real property involves formalities – contract must be evidenced in writing (s 4 Statute of Frauds) or there must be part performance.
If no contract, may be an interest arising from equitable estoppel on principles of Waltons Stores case.
The actual conveyance of the property must be by written instrument (s 34 PLA) and also must be by deed (s 33(1) PLA). Transfer forms lodged at Landgate are deemed to be deeds. 
A legal interest in real property is not is created in until registration at Landgate takes place: s 58 TLA. This confers indefeasibility of title: s 68(1) TLA, although some non registered interests enjoy an exception to indefeasibility of title rule: see s 68(1A) TLA.

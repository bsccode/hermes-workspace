SHORT ANALYSIS (IRAC)
- Issue: Whether a binding contract exists and whether equitable remedies and protection (caveat) are available given the Vendor’s subsequent dealing with a third party.
- Rule: A contract for sale of land must be sufficiently certain as to parties, land, price and essential terms and be in writing where required. Equity recognises that a binding contract creates an equitable proprietary interest in the purchaser. Under the Torrens system, registration governs legal title (Transfer of Land Act 1893 (WA) ss 58, 68) but equitable rights may be protected by caveat. Priority between competing equitable interests is governed by equitable principles (Rice v Rice (1853) 161 ER 646; Heid v Reliance Finance Corp Pty Ltd (1983) 154 CLR 326; Barry v Heider (1914) 19 CLR 197).
- Application: The contract sets out parties, price, property, deposit, settlement date and is written. The Purchaser has paid a deposit of $120,000. The finance and inspection conditions are stated. Provided signatures are obtained and conditions are satisfied or waived, a binding contract and thus an equitable interest will arise. The Vendor’s later sale to a third party will not defeat Wednesday’s equitable interest if the Vendor cannot show the contract was avoided or that the purchaser had notice and priority under the Transfer of Land Act.
- Conclusion: The Purchaser will have an equitable proprietary interest upon formation of the contract and is entitled to equitable remedies (including specific performance) and protection (caveat) until title is transferred at settlement.

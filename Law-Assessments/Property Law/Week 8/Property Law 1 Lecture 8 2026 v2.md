## Slide 3

When 2 or more people claim inconsistent rights over the same property, there is a priorities dispute. 
Because property is a bundle of rights, ownership of different rights might be harmonious (eg a lease where the landlord owns the reversionary interest and the tenant has the immediate right to possession) or they may clash (where the landlord sells the reversionary interest to another who also expects to get possession not knowing about the tenant who has that possessory right).

Where there is a dispute between 2 legal interests: earlier legal (L1) vs later legal (L2):
The general common law principle is that legal interests are good against all the world.
Priority of time of creation of the respective interests is determinative. 
In general, subject to considerations arising out of the conduct of the parties, as between two interests of the same quality, the first in time prevails: Moffett v Dylan [1999] 2 VR 480.

## Slide 11

A prior (registered) legal interest will generally prevail over a later (unregistered) equitable interest 
However, a legal interest will be postponed to a subsequent equitable interest where the owner of the legal estate has been involved in a fraud which created the later equitable interest by concealing the prior legal interest giving the impression that the equitable estate would be a legal estate.
Northern Counties of England Fire Insurance Co v Whipp (1884) 26 Ch D 482.
A manager of a company borrowed money from the company and mortgaged his property to them. The title deeds were kept in the company safe. The manager then took the title deeds out of the safe (he had access to the key) and borrowed money from Mrs Whipp who became equitable mortgagee without notice of the prior legal mortgage. 
Mrs Whip sought a declaration that the earlier legal mortgage was fraudulent and should be postponed because of conduct of company as legal mortgagee in not retaining possession of title deeds. The court held that the legal mortgage had priority and would not be postponed. This was because the company, while careless in respect of their control over the deeds, was not party to the fraud nor did it hold out that the manager (the mortgagor) was its agent. Per Fry J:
(1) ‘where the owner of the legal estate has assisted in or connived at the fraud which has led to the creation of a subsequent equitable estate, without notice of the prior legal estate’; or
(2) ‘where the owner of the legal estate has constituted the mortgagor his agent to raise money and the estate thus created has by the fraud or misconduct of the agent been represented as being the first estate.’ 
However, ‘the court will not postpone the prior legal estate to the subsequent equitable estate on the ground of mere carelessness or want of prudence on the part of the legal owner.’
Land under the Torrens system has similar rules (exceptions to indefeasibility of title) – discussed later.

## Slide 12

The general law requirements for the later legal interest to prevail: 
The purchaser of the legal interest must be bona fide and for value.
The purchaser must take without notice of the prior equitable interest. The 3 types of notice are actual, imputed and constructive notice.
Actual notice is self explanatory.
Imputed notice is where notice is imputed to the holder. For example, notice given to a purchaser’s agent is imputed to the purchaser.	
As to determining constructive notice, the purchaser has two duties:
A duty to search and examine all documents pertaining to the transaction.
A duty to inspect the land – eg Jared v Clements [1902] 2 Ch 399, Smith v Jones [1954] 1 WLR 1089.

## Slide 14

Rice v Rice (1853) 161 ER 646
This case involved a prior vendor’s equitable lien vs subsequent equitable mortgagee.
The vendor had sold his property to a relative and signed a receipt for the purchase price even though money hadn’t been paid. This was to enable the purchaser (the relative) to borrow against the property from a bank (who believed he owed nothing on the property). The purchaser borrowed from a bank and gave them the title deeds effectively giving the bank an equitable mortgage. The vendor claimed his equitable interest (because he was entitled to be paid) prevailed. The court found that the later bank interest should prevail as the conduct of the vendor had put them in this position so he only had himself to blame.

## Slide 15

Rice v Rice (1853) 161 ER 646 (Kindersley VC):
The general rule of equity as to priorities is that as between persons having only equitable interests, if their equities are in all other respects equal, priority of time gives the better equity. In order to determine if the equities are equal, the court will generally consider:
[1] the nature and condition of the  respective equitable interests; 
[2] the circumstances and manner of acquisition of the respective interests; and 
[3] the whole conduct of each party.

## Slide 16

Barry v Heider (1914) 19 CLR 197
Barry signed a transfer of property over to Schmidt who didn’t pay the stated amount of £1,200. Schmidt mortgaged the property to Heider. Heider did not register the mortgage but had no notice of Barry’s interest. Barry sought a declaration that the unregistered mortgage was invalid. 
The court found that Heider had an equitable interest that took priority over Barry’s equitable interest (as he Barry longer had a legal interest).
This case is authority for the following:
The taker of a later equitable interest without notice of an earlier equitable interest can have priority where earlier interest holder has contributed to later interest holder getting their equitable interest.
That an unregistered instrument can confer an equitable interest (in this case a mortgage).

## Slide 17

More stupidity – ‘arming’ the intermediary with the means of granting a subsequent equitable interest.
Abigail v Lapin 51 CLR 58 (1934 PC case on appeal from HC Australia).
The Lapins transferred title to their land to Mrs Heavener as security for a loan from her. She subsequently borrowed money from the Abigails who had no notice of the Lapins’ prior equitable interest. The Abigails did not register their mortgage so only had an equitable interest. 
The court held that the Lapins had ‘armed’ Ms Heavener with the means of entering in the subsequent arrangement by transferring the title to her and not putting a caveat on the title so had postponed their interest to the Abigails.

## Slide 18

HCA’s current position:
Heid v Reliance Finance Corp Pty Ltd (1983) 154 CLR 326:
	Priority between competing equitable interests is to be determined by a more general and flexible principle with preference given to … the better equity. In an examination of the relevant circumstances … the overriding question is … whose is the better equity, bearing in mind the conduct of both parties, the question of any negligence on the part of the prior claimant, the effect of any representation (by the prior claimant) as possibly raising an estoppel (in favour of the later holder) and whether it can be said that the conduct of the … prior owner has enabled such a representation to be made.
This case was similar to Rice v Rice. Heid sold property to a company. He acknowledged receipt of $165K in full payment for the property, but he had only been paid $15K. The company mortgaged the property to Reliance Finance by way of an equitable mortgage. Reliance Finance had no knowledge of Heid’s equitable interest (which was a vendor’s lien). The High Court held that Heid had postponed his prior equitable interest to Reliance Finance’s later equitable interest.

## Slide 19

All things being equal, as a general rule an earlier equitable interest will be postponed to a later one where:
 The conduct of the earlier holder has allowed the later holder to acquire that interest in the mistaken belief that the earlier interest did not exist (Barry v Heider).
 The earlier holder’s conduct contributed to the later holder acquiring its interest without notice of the earlier interest (Abigailv Lapin).
 Where it was reasonably foreseeable that as a consequence of the earlier holder’s conduct, a later equitable interest might be created and the owner of the later interest would assume the non-existence of the earlier (Heid v Reliance Finance).

## Slide 20

What is an equitable interest? What we have looked at thus far – eg an equitable freehold interest acquired in land pursuant to a contract of sale that has not been transferred to the purchaser, an equitable leasehold interest, an interest held by a beneficiary of an express or implied trust – in other words, a proprietary equitable interest
What is a mere equity? This has been hard for courts to define, but it involves an interest which has not yet matured into an equitable interest eg the right of an innocent party to seek the assistance of a court to set aside a voidable contract tainted by fraud or undue influence.
The priority rule here is that, provided the equitable interest has been acquired in good faith and for valuable consideration and without notice, it will prevail over the mere equity.
Latec Investments v Hotel Terrigal (1965) 113 CLR 265

## Slide 21

Priority rules for Torrens title land (this will make more sense once we have completed lectures on the Torrens system).
Earlier legal versus later legal – priority simply depends on the date of registration.
Earlier equitable versus later legal – the doctrine of notice is abolished except in the case of fraud (see s 134 TLA – doesn’t matter if there is notice) and instead replaced by the concept of indefeasibility of title, subject to the recognised exceptions (s 68(1), (1A) TLA).
Earlier legal interest versus later equitable interest – the earlier registered interest prevails unless the later unregistered interest is an exception to indefeasibility.
Competing equitable interests – apply the general law rule (eg Rice v Rice) but also consider any failure to lodge a caveat as something that may postpone priority.

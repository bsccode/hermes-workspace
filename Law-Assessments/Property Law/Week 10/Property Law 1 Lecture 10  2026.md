## Slide 3

Under the Torrens system of deed registration, title is given by registering a deed instrument in the register book.
This registration creates the legal interest immediately The title is indefeasible - good against the whole world.
Registration creates the title: it is title by registration (Breskvar v Wall).
Indefeasibility does not apply to: rights in personam, some covenants unconnected to land, and where there are exceptions (this lecture).
s 58 TLA – registration creates legal interest.
s 68(1) TLA – indefeasibility of title – registered interest is only subject to prior registered interests, subject to fraud exception and misdescription of title.
s 68(1A) TLA – sets out other exceptions to indefeasibility (eg rights of way, unpaid rates, mining leases, lease instruments <5 years, etc).
s 85 – every instrument of transfer registered at Landgate deemed to have same effect as a deed.

## Slide 4

Effect of Registration = Immediate indefeasibility of title.
A registered title is conclusive regardless of defects.
Registration gives priority against all, except exceptions that are recognised.
A registered proprietor is not affected by actual or constructive notice of an unregistered interest and has no obligation to inquire into quality of title.
Only those exceptions in the TLA affect a registered interest.
So, the Torrens system displaces the common law doctrine of notice and alters common law rules on priorities.

## Slide 10

What do we mean by fraud?
[F]raud meant actual fraud, i.e. dishonesty of some sort, not what is called constructive or equitable fraud … Further it appears … that the fraud which must be proved in order to invalidate the title of a registered proprietor for value … must be brought home to the person whose registered title is impeached or to his agents. The mere fact that he might have found out fraud if he had been more vigilant and had made further inquiries which he omitted to make, does not of itself prove fraud on his part. But if it be shown that he abstained from making inquiries for fear of learning the truth, the case is very different and fraud may be properly ascribed to him. A person who presents for registration of a document which is forged or has been fraudulently or improperly obtained is not guilty of fraud if he honestly believes it to be a genuine document which can be properly acted upon.
Assets Co Ltd v Mere Roihi [1905] AC 17

## Slide 11

Fraud includes actual ‘personal dishonesty or moral turpitude’ or ‘grave moral blame’. (Stuart v Kingston (1923) HCA 17.
There is a wide variety of forms of fraud eg a misrepresentation by a registered proprietor that he would not override an unregistered interest which he knew about when he got on the title: Loke Yew v Port Swettenham [1913] AC 491).
But note more modern and broader concept of fraud: Bahr v Nicolay (No 2) (1988) 164 CLR 604.

## Slide 12

Mr and Mrs Bahr were unable to raise money to develop their land. To finance redevelopment they sold land to Nicolay on terms that they might lease land for a number of years and then buy it back for amount specified in cl.6 of contract of sale. Nicolay sold the land to Thompson and this contract included an acknowledgement of the agreement between the Bahrs and Nicolay (cl 4). Thompson said to the Bahrs that he recognised cl. 6 of their contract with Nicolay and would agree to sell land back to them for specified price. However, Thompson then refused to sell to the Bahrs.
The Bahrs brought claim against Nicolay and Thompson under the personal equity AND fraud as exceptions to indefeasibility of title.
The Bahrs’ claim was dismissed by a single judge and then the Full Court of WA Supreme Court. However, they successfully appealed to High Court to establish that their option to purchase conferred on them an equitable interest which Thompson took his interest subject to because he had notice of it, and his refusal to let the Bahrs exercise the option was fraud.
The High Court considered ss 68 & 134 TLA (s 134 provides that a purchaser is not required to inquire into title, and is not affected by notice of unregistered interest … except in the case of fraud: s 134(a)).

Bahr v Nicolay (No 2) (1988) 164 CLR 604

---

## Slide 13

The High Court held that Thompson’s conduct of gave rise to a personal equity enforceable by Bahrs. 
Mason & Dawson JJ: if cl 4 only amounted to notice to Thompson, then the Bahrs’ equitable interest would have been destroyed by registration. However, here there was an agreement or undertaking by Thompson to recognise the Bahrs’ rights.
The issue was one of constructive trust or, if not, that the statutory exception of fraud applied (as fraud could arise if there was an intent to defraud at the time Thompson gave the undertaking or, subsequently, he intended to destroy the prior equitable interest.)
Wilson & Toohey JJ: There was a constructive trust between the Bahrs and Thompson. The Bahrs had an equitable interest in land arising out of the undertaking, but the statutory exception of fraud did not apply.
Brennan J:. A constructive trust should be imposed on Thompson by equity, so Thompson cannot ‘deny this unregistered interest to which he has undertaken to subject his registered title’.
Hence, Bahr v Nicolay (No 2) provides the more modern and broader concept of fraud. The majority saw fraud as bound up in not only actual fraud, but also dishonest conduct and moral turpitude. Three judges thought fraud did not apply here, but two did. But all judges were prepared to impose a constructive trust to protect the prior interest.

Bahr v Nicolay (No 2) (1988) 164 CLR 604

## Slide 15

So mere knowledge of a prior unregistered interest does not automatically mean there will be fraud if registration then occurs which defeats this unregistered interest. In Waimiha Sawmilling Co v Wai One Timber Co [1926] AC 101, the court held that knowledge of a right and a legal challenge to enforce that right is not enough to be fraud. However, the court also said in that case:
If the designated object of a transfer be to cheat a man of a known existing right, that is fraudulent, and so also … [is] a deliberate and dishonest trick causing an interest not to be registered and thus fraudulently keeping the register clear.
What about knowledge of fraud? The courts will focus on the intent of the purchaser, rather than their actual knowledge: see eg Munro v Stuart (1924) 41 SR (NSW) 203. It boils down to whether it amounts to dishonesty to act on such knowledge. 
Note also that s 134 says ‘except in the case of fraud’ so knowledge of an out and out fraud might not invoke the protection of s 134.

## Slide 16

For a WA case, see eg LHK Nominees Pty Ltd v Kenworthy (2002) 26 WAR 517 – indefeasible title is not defeasible merely because a party became registered with knowledge that a transfer was drawn in breach of trust.
	On fraud by an agent, see Schultz v Corwill Properties Pty Ltd (1969) 90 WN (NSW) 529. Where fraud is committed by the agent acting within the scope of actual or apparent authority, then the fraud is imputed to the principal. 
	Fraud is only operative when induces detrimental action on the part of an innocent party: see Bank of SA v Ferguson (1998) 151 ALR 729. If the defrauded party was motivated by other factors (that is, they would have still done the deal despite the fraud) then the fraud not operative.

## Slide 18

Arise out of common law, not statute.
The in personam exception – indefeasibility does not affect the personal obligations of a registered proprietor. You might not have a right against the property they sold (a right in rem), but at least you have a right against them! (a right in personam).
The principle of indefeasibility ‘in no way denies the right of a plaintiff to bring against a registered proprietor a claim in personam, founded in law or in equity, for such relief as a court acting in personam may grant’: Frazer v Walker (1967) 1 AC 569.
A right in personam must arise from the acts of the registered proprietor or of someone for whom he is legally responsible. 
The leading case is Bahr v Nicolay (No 2) – this case illustrates that unlike the fraud exception which is only in respect of conduct prior to registration (where you are trying to impugn title), the in personam exception can arise from the conduct of the registered proprietor either before or after registration (as the goal is not to impugn title, just to get damages).

## Slide 19

Not defined in the TLA.
A notice or caution recorded to protect an unregistered interest. 
Its main function is protective. 
It operates as a claim by someone that he/she shall receive notice before any dealing with the land is carried through, and it will either:
forbid registration of any further interests that may affect the interest recorded in the caveat without the caveator’s consent (absolute caveat); or
dealing is permitted to go ahead subject to the caveator’s claim (subject to claim caveat).
A caveat is a notice or warning and although it must support an interest in land (a caveatable interest) it does not in itself create an interest.

## Slide 21

Sometimes failure to register a caveat is relevant, sometimes it is  irrelevant. 
For example: Will a failure to lodge a caveat be seen as conduct that postpones your equitable interest to a later equitable interest?
A number of factors will be considered to decide, in fairness and justice, whether the failure means that an unregistered equitable interest cannot take priority over a subsequent registered (legal) interest; Jacobs v Platt Nominees case.

## Slide 22

Facts: 
Parties entered contract for sale of rural land in NSW. The purchaser made a deposit and signed the contract. On the day of settlement, the purchaser was informed that the vendor owed a third party some monies. The purchaser obtained a final search of the title in the morning but didn’t look again in the afternoon when a writ of execution under civil procedure legislation (not caveat) was lodged. The settlement was stopped.
HELD: 
The writ of execution defeated interests of the purchaser (ie equitable interest created by attachment of property due to judgment - a writ of execution, although registered, only creates an equitable interest in the property - versus an earlier equitable interest in property due to contract of sale). The purchaser should have lodged a caveat to protect their interest.Further, the settlement agent should have checked just before settlement as they knew money was owed to a third party.

Black v Garnock (2007) 230 CLR 438

## Slide 23

L1 v L2 – priority depends on the date of registration. Subject to exceptions, a registered interest is subject to any earlier registered interest.
E1 v L2 – the doctrine of notice is abolished and indefeasibility applies, subject only to the recognised exceptions 
L1 v E2 – L1 prevails unless L1 can be impugned by the fraud exception to indefeasibility (fraud broadly defined).
E1 v E2 – equitable interests are more vulnerable under the TLA because of the abolition of the doctrine of notice – s 134 TLA. However, s 134 TLA cannot be relied on if there is actual or constructive notice of fraud, and this notice sheets home to the purchaser some form of wrongdoing. Priority depends upon the general priority rules and who has the better equity (see eg Rice v Rice), but there is an additional matter to consider under the TLA – has there been a failure to lodge a caveat and, if so, is it postponing conduct?

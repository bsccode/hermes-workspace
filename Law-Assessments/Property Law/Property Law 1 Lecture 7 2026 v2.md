## Slide 7

In transfer of land by contract, courts will, in the absence of legal requirements, treat the purchaser as owner of the equitable interest
BUT: Not so for gifts.
General Rule: Equity will decline a remedy for donee if donor fails to take the steps necessary to transfer legal title in the subject matter.
Applying two maxims:1. Equity does not assist a volunteer: Jones v Lock (1865) 1 Ch App 25.
2. Equity will not perfect an imperfect gift: Milroy v Lord (1862) 4 De GF & J 264. The donor must do everything necessary to be done to transfer title in light of the nature of the property.

## Slide 8

Milroy v Lord (1862) 4 De GF & J 264
The donor wanted to establish a trust of shares for his niece. He executed a deed of trust but prior to his death did not execute a transfer which the memorandum of association of the company required him to sign and register to properly effect a transfer of the shares to the niece. The niece was entitled to the dividends of the shares but not the shares themselves as a registered signed transfer was necessary to perfect the gift to her.
At 1189-1190 (Turner LJ) said: 
[I]n order to render a voluntary settlement (gift) valid and effectual, the settlor must have done everything, which, according to the nature of the property  comprised in the settlement, was necessary to be done in order to transfer the property and render the settlement binding upon him…

## Slide 9

Corin v Patton (1990) 169 CLR 540: 
A dying wife, Mrs Patton, tried to sever joint tenancy with her husband, from whom she had separated, by purporting to transfer her interest to her brother to hold on trust for her children pursuant to a deed of trust. However, Mrs Patton had not arranged for the Certificate of Title to be produced which was required for lodgement for registration of the transfer.
HELD: Applying the principle in Milroy v Lord, the gift could not be regarded as complete in equity without the production of the CT – there was only a statement of intention which was not enough. Equity will not perfect an imperfect gift.

## Slide 10

BUT see: 
Re Ward; Billet v Ward [1968] WAR 33: 
A donor before his death instructed his solicitor to transfer pieces of land to his son. The donor and donee executed a transfer and the donor gave the solicitor an authority for his bank to deliver the certificate of title to the solicitors so that they could register the transfer. The donee agreed to pay the stamp duty, but couldn’t do so immediately. The donor died before the stamp duty was paid and before the transfer was registered.
HELD:  The solicitors were holding the transfer and bank authority on behalf of donee who had agreed to pay duty.
So, here the donor’s gift was complete as the donor had done all that was necessary for the donor to transfer property. (The obligation to pay stamp duty was not on the donor but the donee.)
Lesson from all this: Every case rests on its facts. ‘It depends’.

## Slide 11

Express Trusts
A trust is where the holder of legal or equitable interest creates a trust of that property for another’s benefit.
There are many reasons why one would create a trust, eg tax minimisation; charitable purposes, superannuation, and to hold property for those unable to do so (eg under 18).
Trusts separate (fragment) the legal and equitable title to assets.
If a living person creates a trust by declaration, the equitable interest in property passes to the beneficiaries but the trustee holds the legal interest.
Trusts must be documented.

## Slide 14

Implied Trusts (trusts implied at law)
Resulting trusts – Arise when there are contributions to purchase property. Someone who has legal title to property is presumed to hold that property on resulting trust for those who provide the purchase money (or have contributed part of the purchase money). The beneficial interest in the property is proportionate to the amount contributed, which ‘results back’ to the person who paid. This presumption is known as the presumption of resulting trust.
However, this presumption may be rebutted by evidence that the contribution was a gift.
(Also note here a separate presumption: the presumption of advancement. This presumption arises in certain situations, such as contributions from parents to children. Here, it is presumed that a contribution to purchasing property is intended as a gift. Once again, this presumption is rebuttable by evidence that the contributor wanted an interest in the property.)

## Slide 15

Constructive trusts – Where a person gains an equitable interest in the property in circumstances where it would be unfair to deny that person an interest. 
In Olgilvie v Ryan [1976] 2 NSWLR 504, Ogilvie promised Ryan that the house would be hers if she looked after him. He died and did not mention Ryan in his will. The executor (Ogilvie’s son) took action to remove Ryan from the house, but didn’t succeed as it would have been unconscionable for the executor to take the  property without recognising Ryan’s interest.

The use of resulting trusts and constructive trusts was important in de facto relationships where marriage laws did not apply and one person was on the title but both contributed to the property either financially or non-financially.
Of course, family law legislation now governs the distribution of property rights between spouses and de factos (of 2 years or more standing) and takes into account non-financial contributions as well as financial contributions to the relationship.
